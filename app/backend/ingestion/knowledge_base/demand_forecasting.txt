Demand Forecasting in Supply Chain Management

Demand forecasting predicts future customer demand for products so that supply chain decisions — procurement, production, inventory, and logistics — can be made proactively rather than reactively.

Forecasting Methods:

1. Statistical Models
Time-series methods such as ARIMA, Exponential Smoothing (ETS), and Holt-Winters decompose historical demand into trend, seasonality, and noise components to project future demand. These are fast and interpretable but struggle with sudden market shifts.

2. Machine Learning Forecasting
Gradient boosting models (XGBoost, LightGBM) and deep learning models (LSTM, Temporal Fusion Transformer) capture complex non-linear patterns in demand data. They can ingest external signals like promotions, weather, and economic indicators.

3. Collaborative Forecasting (CPFR)
Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment involves sharing point-of-sale data and promotional calendars between retailers and suppliers to produce a single agreed-upon forecast.

4. Consensus Forecasting
Combines statistical forecasts with market intelligence, sales pipeline data, and management judgment through a structured Sales and Operations Planning (S&OP) process.

The Bullwhip Effect:
Small fluctuations in retail demand are amplified as they propagate upstream through distributors, manufacturers, and raw material suppliers. The bullwhip effect is caused by demand signal processing, order batching, price fluctuations, and shortage gaming. Real-time POS data sharing and vendor-managed inventory (VMI) reduce this distortion.

Forecast Accuracy Metrics:
- Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE): average absolute % error across periods
- Weighted MAPE (WMAPE): weights errors by volume, preventing small SKUs from dominating
- Forecast Bias: systematic over- or under-forecasting
- Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE): penalizes large errors more heavily

Inventory Positioning:
Safety stock = Z-score × σ(demand) × √lead time
Where Z-score corresponds to the desired service level (e.g. 1.65 for 95%).

A-B-C Classification:
- A items (top 20% SKUs, 80% of revenue): tight control, frequent review
- B items (middle tier): moderate control
- C items (long tail): loose control, bulk replenishment
